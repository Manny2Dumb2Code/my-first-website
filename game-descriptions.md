KCD1:

"Kingdom Come Deliverance begins not with a hero, but with a boy. You are experiencing the life of an ordinary young man in a world so vivid and alive that you don't just play through it, you live within it. Merchants at their stalls. Guards on patrol. Gamblers hunched over a game of farkle. A castle in the distance you desperately want to explore. This is Skalitz — and for a brief, beautiful moment, it feels permanent.
Then the piercing sound of a flaming arrow hits the ground and a distant horn stops all movement. In the next few moments everything Henry has ever known is swallowed by fire, steel and screaming. Cuman mercenaries flood the streets, turning life to ash. From the safety of the castle gates, you are forced to watch a slaughter you are powerless to stop. You watch Markvart von Aulitz cut down Henry's parents while Henry remains paralyzed. Unable to move. Unable to help.
It is that paralysis that haunts Henry for the rest of the game. Not the grief — the shame. The suffocating belief that things would have been different if he had been stronger, braver, capable. What follows is not simply a revenge story. It is the story of a boy determined to become someone his parents would have survived to see. Through blood and steel, through diplomatic courts and dangerous missions, through failure and growth, Henry molds himself into the person he needed to be on the day he lost everything.
Kingdom Come Deliverance understands something most games never attempt — that a man's hardest battles are never fought with a sword. They are fought with grief, guilt and the relentless, quiet need to prove that the people who loved you did not die for nothing.

RDR2:

"Red Dead Redemption 2 presents Arthur Morgan — a man shaped entirely by the ruthless world that claimed him as an orphan and the outlaw father figure who molded him into a killer. Arthur exists in a brutal duality; the same hands that shoot a man dead are the hands that patiently teach a young boy to fish, sharing a rare moment of peace in a world that allows him very little. A man convinced that harming the innocent is right, if it means protecting the only family he's ever known, is soon confronted by the weight of his actions, and that internal battle is where RDR2 truly breaks you. For Arthur is just one man in a dying world — a eulogy for a vanishing era, a story about outlaws clinging to a way of life as civilization tightens its grip around them, and a devastating portrait of what happens when the man you built your entire existence around slowly reveals himself to be everything he claimed to stand against. When it's over, you won't remember the gunfights or the heists — you'll remember a dying man choosing, for the first time in his life, who he truly wanted to be."

 GTA IV:

"Grand Theft Auto IV is not the GTA you remember. At its heart is Niko Bellic — a man hollowed out by war, who crosses an ocean chasing revenge against the man who betrayed him on a blood soaked battlefield. Cold, self reliant and brutally pragmatic, Niko accepts dirty work without hesitation if it funds his mission and moves him closer to his target. Killing a man and leaving his blood to spill over city pavement was just as easy as spilling it in the cold dirt from the war back home. And yet beneath that hardened exterior lives a man capable of genuine loyalty — to his cousin Roman, to those who earned his trust, to the few who saw something worth caring about in him. Those who met Niko were either opportunities, comrades or enemies — with nothing in between. He came to America chasing a dream that was never real — and what he found instead was the oldest truth there is: that the past doesn't stay where you left it."

The Last of Us:

"The Last of Us is not a story about a fungal apocalypse. It is a story about a father shaped by the worst moment a human being can endure — watching his daughter die in his arms in a world that was supposed to protect them both. The Joel we meet years later is hardened, cunning and emotionally buried. When he is tasked with transporting Ellie — a stubborn, distrustful orphaned girl who reminds him of everything he lost — he doesn't see a child. He sees a liability. He sees a wound reopening. What happens between them across the ruins of a collapsed civilization is not a mission. It is two broken people unconsciously giving each other exactly what they needed most — Ellie finding the father she never had, and Joel finding a reason to be one again. By the end, you won't question a single choice he makes. You'll understand exactly why a father does what a father does. Think about someone you love deeply and ask yourself — if you lost them, would the world still make sense? If you're anything like Joel, then his story isn't just unforgettable. It's yours.

 Fallout 4:

"Fallout 4 begins not with action, but with paralysis. You watch helplessly — frozen, voiceless, trapped behind glass — as an armed stranger takes your son from the arms of your lifeless wife, a mother who died trying to protect your child, and in one moment, the only two things you had left in a cruel world that was already awaiting you, were gone. When you finally emerge from your cryogenic chamber into a poorly lit, haunting vault, the silence around every corner feels heavier than any enemy lurking beyond it. And when you step outside — into a world bombed into memory, where buildings are now walls, trees are now skeletons, and neighborhoods that once held life and color are now debris scattered across dead earth — the realization hits you like the blast itself. You are 210 years too late. Everything is ancient. Everything is gone. What follows is a desperate, paranoid crawl through a world that wants you dead — through dense cities, broken highways, suburban graveyards and the terrifying Glowing Sea, where radioactive clouds swallow visibility whole and lizard like creatures with glowing green veins stalk the darkness. Every survivor you meet, every faction that offers help, every mission that pulls you sideways — all of it stands between you and your son. And when you finally find him, the truth that awaits you is the kind of plot twist that doesn't just surprise you. It numbs you."


Skyrim:

Skyrim begins in darkness—accompanied by the sound of a whistling wind as anticipation builds. What follows is a cold awakening to the merciless world that awaits you. You are a prisoner, unnamed and unheard, marching toward a chopping block in a frost-bitten village where some mourn your fate and others eagerly await it. The executioner works with the cold indifference of a woodsman—severing heads from necks like a branch off a log. And yours is next. Then the dragon comes.

What follows is one of gaming's greatest openings—chaos, fire, and the sudden, violent reminder that in Skyrim, the world does not wait for you to be ready. You flee through burning streets, dodging the dragon’s incinerating roar, until you claw your way through the darkness of an underground passage and break back into the open air. The sudden silence is jarring, and the scale of what awaits is humbling: snowy mountains that pierce the clouds, rivers roaring through ancient canyons, and rolling hills draped in vibrant, wild flora. You feel impossibly small.

But Skyrim's greatest lesson isn't found in a quest log. It is felt in every step through a world that conceals its teeth behind breathtaking beauty—for every delicate flower on the ground, something ominous lurks nearby to destroy it. You learn quickly that kindness can be a trap, that a man in distress will still betray you the moment you free him, and that a lie can become truth, depending on who tells it.

And yet, the Dragonborn—this prisoner the world tried to execute—becomes its shield. To protect a world that betrayed, deceived, and sought to destroy you is a rare kind of strength. That is what makes Skyrim unforgettable. Not the dragons. Not the shouting. But the quiet, stubborn heroism of someone who had every reason to let it all burn.


Bully:

Bully is Rockstar's most underrated story — and perhaps their most human. At its center is Jimmy Hopkins, a brash, guarded and fiercely righteous kid dropped into a world of structured cruelty, where jocks, greasers and preppy elites believe their status grants them the right to treat others however they please. Jimmy doesn't just ignore their hierarchies — he dismantles them. In his world there is only right and wrong, and it is his scuffed knuckles that deliver the verdict. But beneath its rebellious surface, Bully teaches something profound — that courage means standing up for those who won't stand up for themselves, that authority doesn't equal integrity, and that everyone, from the school bully to the alcoholic teacher hiding behind a professional exterior, is just a person struggling through life. We all bruise the same. Jimmy Hopkins understood that before most adults ever do. In a game about schoolyard fights and stolen bikes, Rockstar quietly made one of the most honest arguments for human decency ever put into an interactive medium.
